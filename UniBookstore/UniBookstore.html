University Book Store

Redesign of Course Materials View and Checkout Process

Duration- April 2021, 2 weeks
Type- Solo project
Context- Course assignment (HCDE301)
Skills- Ui/Ux design and heuristics analysis

Assignment Prompt

Use heuristics to do a usability analysis on your website of choice, then redesign it accordingly. I chose the University of Washington’s University Book Store’s website, and focused on the experience of viewing course materials and the check out process.

Project Goal

Every year, more than 40,000 students attend classes at Seattle’s University of Washington. Many order class materials from the University Bookstore. Is the UI for online purchase effective in allowing users to purchase needed course materials?

Heuristics used for Analysis

I used heuristics from Nielsen’s list of 10 usability heuristics and a heuristics presented on the Laws of Ux website (lawsofux.com). Here are the three heursitics I used:

Recognition/Recall

Features should have clear (visual) cues so users don’t need to memorize how to use it. This is especially important because the user base is mostly students who only purchase course materials 3-4 times a year, and because the user pool constantly changes as students graduate.

Goal Gradient effect

The existence of a progress marker and goal pushes the user to complete tasks. A study by R. Kivetz, O. Urminsky, and Y. Zheng found that participants who started closer to the goal of 25,000 frequent flyer points had a 47% increase in completion rates over participants who started x6 further away from the 25,000 points.

Hick’s law

The Hick’s Law heuristic states that a greater number of choices is results in a greater amount of time needed to make a decision. Furthermore, there should never be redundant choices as it would increase reaction time without any meaningful reason.

Findings

Analyzing the website according to these heuristics, this is what I found:

Recognition/Recall

Current design requires memorization of specific course code, section, and instructor name.

Goal Gradient effect

Visual cues for progression is too subtle. The list of selected courses is hidden at first, which obfuscates the ‘end goal’ of this page.

Hick’s law

The layout of this page forces the user to make extraneous decisions. The page has two “continue” buttons, one following a vertical flow and the other a horizontal flow. Furthermore, the redundancy of the buttons confuses the user on whether both buttons do the same thing.

Recommendations

Here are several recommendations on how to better apply the aforementioned heuristics to the design.

Recognition/Recall

  • -Adding an “import courses” button where students can sign in with their net ID, and the system will retrieve their data and automatically fill in the form.
  • -Allowing users to search for classes and pick from choices with a more detailed description such as course title and schedule.

Goal Gradient effect

  • -Increasing visibility of progress in the “Select Your Courses” form.
  • -Reveal the ‘end-goal’ of the “Select Your Courses” page; always show the “Selected Courses” list.

Hick’s law

  • -Remove the redundant button in the payment processing page.
